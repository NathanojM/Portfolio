Background

According to the 2021 census, there were 374,000 international students in the UK in 2021 (ONS, 2023) who make up a large part of the UK's vibrant multicultural society. It is estimated that international students in the 2021/ 2022 academic year made a net contribution of 37 billion pounds to our economy (HEPI, 2023). However, the distribution of origins and destinations of students are not even, and understanding where they come from and where they go (both domestically and internationally) is critical to maintaining a quality higher education environment.

Methodology

This analyses uses data from (HESA, 2024), who collect statistics on higher education. They provide an origin-destination matrix for international students studying in the UK.
This was converted to a list of student 'transactions' using a Python script (function 1).

Results

These maps show the flows of students from countries around the world in orthogonal projections, with identical scale values. Most of the flows are reasonably similar, but the top three (India, China and Nigeria) dwarf any other country. This conflicts slightly with the 2021 census data (ONS, 2023) which has places Nigeria in fourth place after Romania. This suggests large year on year changes to student migration patterns.

Figure 1:10 shows flows from the UK's nearest neighbours to specific UK universities. It shows a clear preference from Irish students for Durham, Birmingham City and Brighton universities, and a large flow from Germany to various London universities. The large volumes of students flowing to London supports the findings of (ONS, 2023) who reported that in 2021, a third of the UK's international students were studying in London.

*Figure 1:10*

This shows all the flows in the 2023/24 academic year between counties and universities, to show where the students came from and where they went. There is an interesting trend in Northern Ireland, where there is a clear preference for Ulster University over Queen's or St Mary's.
There is some localisation. For example, there is a large flow across the Scottish Borders between Lanarkshire and Edinburgh. In Aberdeenshire, there's a large flow to the university of Aberdeen.
Showing all the flows on a single map leads to a messy visualisation, but this can be improved by showing only the top 500 flows.

From this, a histogram can be calculated showing how far students travel to university. It is highly skewed, with most students travelling around 200 km. There is a slight bimodal distribution, with a second peak around 600km, which suggests that most students choose either to stay local or study far away, but that few choose the middle ground.

Figure 1:12: Histogram of domestic travel distance

The average direction can also be calculated for each area by calculating the bearing from North and then symbolising it with a vector field marker, to show which direction students generally travel towards. Figure 1:13 shows that the median travel direction is almost unanimously towards the South East. The only students travelling North are those on the south coast, who have nowhere south to travel. There is significant geographical variation in the skew of the data; the mean and median diverge significantly more in the south than in the north, suggesting that there are higher levels of inequality in the south of England than in the North. This is reflected in the UK's IMD statistics, which show more division within cities in the South, and more overwhelmingly deprived areas in the north. (Geographic Data Service, 2019)

(Whittaker, 2012) found some links between student mobility and economic background but found it not to be an overwhelmingly important factor. They suggested (among others) availability of particular subjects in a student's home county to be important factors as well.

It also shows that the mean is not a good method of averaging direction, with the Isle of Wight being the best example. It shows the mean direction as flowing south, even though this is physically impossible because it's on the south coast. This is because there is an approximately equal number of students travelling east and west, which averages out to 180 degrees.

It would appear to be a coincidence that the arrows converge on the Open University in Milton Keynes, as when this was removed from the dataset (because it is a institution built around distance learning), the difference was unnoticeable. It is more likely to be an artefact of the geometry of the UK that flows tend to point to the centre.
